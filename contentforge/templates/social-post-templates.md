# Social Post Templates — ContentForge Social Adapter

**Purpose:** Five proven post frameworks for repurposing article content into social media posts. Each framework includes a template pattern, platform-specific variations, and a worked example.

**Used by:** Social Adapter Agent (`agents/10-social-adapter.md`) and `/cf-social-adapt` skill

---

## Framework 1: Announcement

**When to use:** Launching new content, sharing a key finding, highlighting a trend.

**Core pattern:**
```
[BOLD CLAIM or TREND STATEMENT]

[2-3 sentences of context: why this matters, what changed, what the data shows]

[IMPLICATION for the reader: what should they do, think, or watch for]

[CTA: link, question, or prompt]
```

### Platform Variations

**LinkedIn (800-1,200 chars):**
- Lead with the trend in a single declarative sentence
- Provide 3-5 sentences of data-backed context
- End with an implication for the reader's industry and a question
- Professional tone, no hype

**Twitter/X (200-270 chars):**
- Trend statement + one supporting data point + link
- No room for context -- the stat IS the context
- End with a link or single hashtag

**Instagram (300-600 chars caption):**
- Bold text overlay on visual for the claim
- Caption provides "why it matters" context
- End with "Save this" or "What do you think?"
- 8-12 hashtags at end or first comment

**Facebook (400-700 chars):**
- Conversational opener: "Did you know..." or "Something is shifting in [industry]..."
- Accessible language, less jargon than LinkedIn
- End with a question that invites discussion
- 1-3 hashtags

**Threads (150-300 chars):**
- Hot take format: "[Trend]. Full stop. [Why it matters in one sentence]."
- Conversation prompt at end
- 1-2 hashtags

### Example: Announcement

**Source moment:** "73% of healthcare organizations now use AI-powered diagnostics, up from 12% in 2024"

**LinkedIn:**
```
73% of healthcare organizations now use AI-powered diagnostics.

Two years ago, that number was 12%. This is not gradual adoption -- it is
a complete transformation of how medicine gets practiced.

What is driving the acceleration? Three converging forces: FDA regulatory
clarity, plummeting model costs, and diagnostic accuracy that now exceeds
human specialists by double digits.

For healthcare leaders still in evaluation mode: the window for early-mover
advantage is closing. The question has shifted from "should we adopt?" to
"how fast can we implement safely?"

Full analysis: [link]

#AIinHealthcare #HealthTech #DigitalHealth #PrecisionMedicine
```

**Twitter/X:**
```
73% of healthcare orgs now use AI diagnostics. Two years ago: 12%.

That is not a trend. That is a transformation.

[link]

#AIinHealthcare
```

## Framework 2: Data-Driven Insight

**When to use:** Sharing a specific statistic with analysis. Best for surprising or counterintuitive data.

**Core pattern:**
```
[SPECIFIC DATA POINT — number, percentage, dollar amount]

[CONTEXT — what this number means, why it is significant, what baseline it compares to]

[ANALYSIS — your take on what is driving this, what it implies]

[SO WHAT — what the reader should take away]

[CTA]
```

### Platform Variations

**LinkedIn (800-1,500 chars):**
- Open with the number in its own line (scroll-stopping)
- 4-6 sentences unpacking what the number means
- Compare to baseline or industry average for contrast
- End with "What are you seeing in your organization?"

**Twitter/X (200-270 chars):**
- Number + one-line context + implication
- Thread format if the stat needs unpacking: "Here is why this matters (thread):"
- Single data card image recommended

**Instagram (300-600 chars):**
- Stat as text overlay on visual (large font, branded colors)
- Caption unpacks the "so what"
- Carousel if multiple related stats
- "Which number surprised you most?" engagement hook

**Facebook (400-700 chars):**
- "Here is a number that stopped me: [stat]."
- Accessible explanation of what it means
- Compare to something the audience can relate to
- "What do you think -- does this match what you are seeing?"

**Threads (150-300 chars):**
- "[Stat]. Let that sink in. [One sentence of context]."
- Short, punchy, conversation-ready

### Example: Data-Driven Insight

**Source moment:** "AI diagnostic accuracy now exceeds human radiologists by 14% for early-stage cancers"

**LinkedIn:**
```
AI diagnostic accuracy now exceeds human radiologists by 14% for early-stage
cancer detection.

That is not a marginal improvement. In diagnostic medicine, 14% is the
difference between catching a tumor at Stage 1 versus Stage 3.

The data comes from a 2026 meta-analysis across 47 hospital systems. The
AI systems are not replacing radiologists -- they are catching what tired
eyes miss, especially during overnight shifts and high-volume periods.

The hospitals deploying these tools are not doing it to cut costs. They are
doing it because the malpractice exposure of NOT using a demonstrably
superior tool is becoming untenable.

Where does your organization stand on AI-assisted diagnostics?

#AIdiagnostics #HealthTech #RadiologyAI #PrecisionMedicine #PatientSafety
```

**Twitter/X:**
```
AI outperforms human radiologists by 14% for early-stage cancer detection.

Not marginal. In diagnostics, that is the difference between Stage 1 and Stage 3.

#AIdiagnostics #HealthTech
```

## Framework 3: How-To / Tip

**When to use:** Sharing actionable steps, frameworks, checklists, or practical guidance from the article.

**Core pattern:**
```
[PROBLEM or GOAL the reader has]

[FRAMEWORK or STEPS — numbered, clear, actionable]

[PROOF — who has done this successfully, what results they got]

[CTA — save this, try step 1 today, link for full guide]
```

### Platform Variations

**LinkedIn (800-1,500 chars):**
- Open with the problem or goal: "Evaluating AI diagnostic tools? Here is a framework."
- 3-5 numbered steps with brief explanation each
- One proof point (case study or data)
- "Save this post for your next vendor evaluation."

**Twitter/X (200-270 chars):**
- "How to [goal] in 3 steps:" + brief numbered list
- Or thread: each step gets its own tweet
- End with "Full guide: [link]"

**Instagram (carousel format):**
- Slide 1: "How to [goal] in [X] Steps" (cover)
- Slides 2-5: One step per slide with brief explanation
- Final slide: CTA (save, follow, link in bio)
- Caption summarizes and adds context

**Facebook (400-700 chars):**
- Conversational opener: "If you are struggling with [problem], here is what works."
- Steps in a list format (dash or number)
- "Tried this? Let us know how it went."

**Threads (150-350 chars):**
- "Quick tip: [actionable advice in 1-2 sentences]."
- One supporting proof point
- "Try this and let me know what happens."

### Example: How-To / Tip

**Source moment:** "Three steps to evaluate AI diagnostic tools: accuracy benchmarks, integration requirements, compliance checklist"

**LinkedIn:**
```

## Framework 4: Quote Highlight

**When to use:** Featuring a memorable or provocative statement from the article or its sources. Works well for thought leadership positioning.

**Core pattern:**
```
[QUOTABLE STATEMENT — bold, memorable, slightly provocative]

[ATTRIBUTION — who said it, or the context it comes from]

[YOUR TAKE — 2-3 sentences of commentary or analysis]

[CTA — agree/disagree, question, link]
```

### Platform Variations

**LinkedIn (600-1,000 chars):**
- Quote in its own paragraph (no quotation marks needed if it is a paraphrased insight)
- Attribution with credibility markers (institution, role)
- Your analysis: why you agree, what it means for the industry
- "Do you agree? I would love to hear dissenting views."

**Twitter/X (200-270 chars):**
- Quote + attribution + one-line reaction
- Quote card image with the quote text (branded template)
- Engagement question: "Agree or disagree?"

**Instagram (300-500 chars):**
- Quote card as primary visual (bold text on branded background)
- Caption adds context and personal take
- "Double tap if you agree" or "Drop your take in the comments"

**Facebook (400-600 chars):**
- Wrap the quote in a relatable context: "This statement stopped me..."
- Explain why it resonates
- "What is your take?"

**Threads (150-300 chars):**
- Quote + one-line hot take
- "Agree or disagree?" to drive replies

### Example: Quote Highlight

**Source moment:** "The question is no longer whether to adopt AI in healthcare, but how fast you can implement it"

**LinkedIn:**
```
"The question is no longer whether to adopt AI in healthcare, but
how fast you can implement it without compromising patient safety."

This framing captures exactly where the industry stands in 2026.

The debate is over. 73% adoption, 14% accuracy advantage over human
specialists, $4.2 billion in annual savings. The evidence is overwhelming.

The remaining question -- implementation speed versus patient safety --
is the right question. And the organizations answering it well are the
ones building compliance frameworks BEFORE deployment, not after.

What is the biggest barrier to AI adoption in your organization?

#AIinHealthcare #HealthTech #DigitalHealth
```

**Twitter/X:**
```
"The question is no longer whether to adopt AI in healthcare, but how fast."

73% adoption. 14% accuracy advantage. $4.2B annual savings.

The debate is over. Implementation speed is the differentiator now.

#AIinHealthcare
```

## Framework 5: Story / Case Study

**When to use:** Sharing a real-world example with a narrative arc (problem, solution, result). Highest credibility and engagement for proof-oriented audiences.

**Core pattern:**
```
[PROBLEM — what challenge did the organization face?]

[SOLUTION — what did they do? (keep it specific)]

[RESULT — what measurable outcome did they achieve?]

[LESSON — what can the reader learn from this?]

[CTA]
```

### Platform Variations

**LinkedIn (800-1,500 chars):**
- Full narrative arc with specific numbers
- Name the organization (credibility)
- 3-4 bullet results
- "What would this kind of improvement mean for your organization?"

**Twitter/X (200-270 chars):**
- "[Company] had [problem]. They [action]. Result: [outcome]."
- Thread for full story: tweet 1 = problem, tweet 2 = solution, tweet 3 = result
- End with "Full case study: [link]"

**Instagram (300-600 chars):**
- Carousel: Slide 1 = Company + Problem, Slide 2-3 = Solution steps, Slide 4 = Results, Slide 5 = Your takeaway
- Or single image with key result metric as text overlay
- Caption tells the abbreviated story

**Facebook (400-800 chars):**
- Storytelling format: "[Company] had a problem..."
- Conversational, accessible language
- End with: "Have you seen similar results? Share your experience."

**Threads (150-350 chars):**
- "[Company] cut [metric] by [amount] using [approach]. Here is what they did differently."
- Short, punchy, conversation-starting

### Example: Story / Case Study

**Source moment:** "Cleveland Clinic reduced diagnostic wait times by 78% after implementing AI triage"

**LinkedIn:**
```
Cleveland Clinic had a problem.

Diagnostic wait times were climbing. Patient satisfaction scores were

## Common Pitfalls

### Pitfall 1: Zero-Value Link Posts

**Bad:**
```
Check out our latest article on AI in healthcare!
[link]
#AI #Healthcare
```

**Why it fails:** Zero value without the click. No one will click a post that does not demonstrate value upfront.

**Fix:** Lead with the insight. Make the post worth reading even if the reader never clicks.

---

### Pitfall 2: Same Post, Every Platform

**Bad:** Copy-paste the same 500-character post to LinkedIn, Twitter, Instagram, Facebook, and Threads.

**Why it fails:** 500 characters is too long for Twitter (280 limit), too short for LinkedIn (misses depth opportunity), has no visual component for Instagram, and misses the conversational tone for Threads.

**Fix:** Adapt the core moment to each platform's native format. The insight can be the same; the delivery must be different.

---

### Pitfall 3: Hashtag Abuse

**Bad (LinkedIn):**
```
#AI #Healthcare #Innovation #Technology #Future #Leadership #Data
#Analytics #Transformation #Digital #Strategy #Growth #Trends #2026
```

**Why it fails:** 14 hashtags on LinkedIn looks spammy. LinkedIn norm is 3-5.

**Fix:** Use platform-specific hashtag counts. Quality over quantity. Each hashtag should be one the audience actually follows.

---

### Pitfall 4: Weak Hooks

**Bad:**
```
We are excited to share some interesting findings about AI in healthcare.
```

**Why it fails:** "Excited to share" is filler. "Interesting findings" is vague. Nothing stops the scroll.

**Fix:** Lead with the most specific, surprising data point: "AI outperforms human radiologists by 14% for early-stage cancer detection."

---

### Pitfall 5: No CTA

**Bad:**
```
AI is transforming healthcare. 73% of organizations now use AI diagnostics.
The future is here.
```

**Why it fails:** Informs but does not activate. No question, no prompt, no link, no reason to engage.

**Fix:** Always end with a call to action: a question, a save prompt, a link with context, or a follow invitation.

---

### Pitfall 6: Over-Promoting, Under-Delivering

**Bad:**
```
This is THE most comprehensive analysis of AI in healthcare you will read this year.
Trust us. Click now. You will not regret it.
```

**Why it fails:** Overpromise without proof. Reads like clickbait. Erodes trust.

**Fix:** Let the data speak. "14 verified sources, zero speculation" is more compelling than superlatives.

## Quality Standards

**Every post generated using these frameworks must meet:**

1. **Self-contained value** -- Reader gains insight without clicking any link
2. **Platform-native format** -- Reads like it was written FOR that platform
3. **Specific over vague** -- Numbers, names, and examples, not platitudes
4. **Strong hook** -- First line stops the scroll
5. **Clear CTA** -- Reader knows what to do next
6. **Brand voice** -- Tone matches the brand profile
7. **Character compliance** -- Under the platform limit, no exceptions
